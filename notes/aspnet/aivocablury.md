#  AI vocabulary to students using a simple principle:

> **In traditional software engineering, we learned terms like Class, Object, Database, API, Thread, and Microservice.**
> **In AI Engineering, we must learn a new vocabulary such as LLM, Embedding, RAG, Agent, Context Window, and MCP.**

Understanding these terms is not about memorizing definitions. It is about understanding how modern AI-powered applications are built.
When a student first learns programming, they encounter variables, functions, classes, and databases. Similarly, when learning AI Engineering, they must become comfortable with concepts such as prompts, tokens, embeddings, vector databases, and agents.
Think about a modern AI application.
A user asks a question.
The question becomes a prompt.
The prompt is processed by a Large Language Model (LLM).
The LLM may retrieve information from a knowledge base using embeddings and a vector database.
An AI agent may decide to call external tools or APIs.
The model uses its context window to understand the conversation.
Finally, the AI generates a response for the user.
Every step in this process introduces new terminology that software engineers must understand.

For example:
* A **Prompt** is like a method parameter supplied to an AI system.
* An **LLM** is like the business engine that processes requests.
* An **Embedding** is a mathematical representation of information.
* A **Vector Database** is a specialized database for storing embeddings.
* **RAG** is a technique that combines search and generation.
* An **AI Agent** is like an intelligent worker capable of planning and executing tasks.
* **MCP (Model Context Protocol)** is becoming a standard mechanism for connecting AI models with tools and external systems.
* **Context Engineering** is the art of providing the right information to the AI at the right time.

As future solution developers, students should not focus only on learning AI tools such as ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini. Tools will continue to evolve.
Instead, focus on understanding the underlying concepts and vocabulary. The engineer who understands APIs can work with any web framework. Similarly, the engineer who understands prompts, embeddings, RAG, agents, and context engineering can work with any AI platform.
The future software engineer will not simply write code. They will design intelligent systems where humans, AI models, agents, databases, APIs, and business workflows collaborate to solve real-world problems.

Therefore, learning AI vocabulary is the first step toward becoming an AI-enabled Solution Developer rather than merely an AI user.
 start. For software engineers, architects, product managers, QA engineers, and business teams working with AI, it helps to expand the vocabulary beyond the basic 10 terms.
## AI Vocabulary for Modern Teams (Extended Edition)

### AI Fundamentals
1. **Artificial Intelligence (AI)**: Tech that enables machines to think and act like humans.
2. **Machine Learning (ML)**: AI that learns from data and improves automatically.
3. **Deep Learning**: Advanced ML using neural networks for complex tasks.
4. **Neural Network**: Systems modeled after the human brain to recognize patterns.
5. **Generative AI**: AI that creates new content like text, images, or videos.
6. **Large Language Model (LLM)**: Models like ChatGPT or Claude that understand and generate language.
7. **AI Agent**: Autonomous programs that perform tasks and make decisions.
8. **Agentic AI**: Self-directed AI that plans, reasons, and executes tasks independently.
9. **Chatbot**: AI that interacts with users in natural language.
10. **Prompt**: The input you give to an AI to generate a response.
11. **Training** – The process of teaching an AI model using large amounts of data.
12. **Inference** – The stage where a trained model makes predictions or generates responses.
13. **Dataset** – A collection of data used to train or evaluate AI models.
14. **Model** – The trained AI system that performs a specific task.
15. **Algorithm** – A set of rules or procedures used to solve problems or process data.
16. **Parameters** – Internal values learned during training that determine model behavior.
17. **Fine-Tuning** – Further training a pre-trained model on specific business data.
18. **Foundation Model** – A large pre-trained model that can be adapted for many tasks.
19. **Transformer** – The neural network architecture behind most modern LLMs.
20. **Token** – Small pieces of text processed by an AI model.

### LLM and Prompt Engineering

21. **Prompt Engineering** – Designing effective instructions for AI systems.
22. **System Prompt** – Hidden instructions defining an AI assistant's behavior.
23. **Context Window** – The amount of information an AI can remember during a conversation.
24. **Zero-Shot Prompting** – Asking AI to perform a task without examples.
25. **One-Shot Prompting** – Providing one example before asking AI to perform a task.
26. **Few-Shot Prompting** – Giving several examples to guide AI responses.
27. **Chain of Thought (CoT)** – Encouraging AI to reason step-by-step.
28. **Role Prompting** – Assigning a role such as "Act as a Software Architect."
29. **Output Formatting** – Asking AI to return JSON, XML, Markdown, tables, etc.
30. **Prompt Template** – A reusable structure for AI interactions.

### RAG and Knowledge Systems

31. **RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)** – Combining AI generation with external knowledge sources.
32. **Embedding** – Numerical representation of text, images, or data.
33. **Vector Database** – Specialized storage for embeddings.
34. **Semantic Search** – Searching by meaning rather than exact keywords.
35. **Knowledge Base** – A collection of documents used by AI systems.
36. **Chunking** – Splitting large documents into smaller sections.
37. **Similarity Search** – Finding content most related to a query.
38. **Vector Index** – Structure that enables fast semantic searches.
39. **Document Retrieval** – Fetching relevant documents before generating answers.
40. **Grounding** – Ensuring AI responses are based on trusted information.


### Agentic AI
41. **Tool Calling** – Allowing AI to invoke external tools or APIs.
42. **Function Calling** – Structured interaction between AI and software functions.
43. **Multi-Agent System** – Multiple AI agents collaborating on tasks.
44. **Planner Agent** – Breaks a complex task into subtasks.
45. **Executor Agent** – Performs assigned tasks.
46. **Supervisor Agent** – Coordinates other agents.
47. **Autonomous Workflow** – AI-driven process requiring minimal human intervention.
48. **Agent Memory** – Mechanism for storing and recalling information.
49. **Reflection** – AI reviewing and improving its own outputs.
50. **Orchestration** – Managing interactions among multiple AI services and agents.


### AI Quality and Reliability
51. **Hallucination** – AI generating incorrect or fabricated information.
52. **Bias** – Systematic unfairness in AI outputs.
53. **Accuracy** – How often AI produces correct results.
54. **Precision** – Percentage of relevant outputs among all outputs.
55. **Recall** – Percentage of relevant items successfully identified.
56. **Evaluation** – Measuring AI performance against benchmarks.
57. **Benchmark** – Standardized test used to compare AI models.
58. **Guardrails** – Controls preventing unsafe or undesirable outputs.
59. **Alignment** – Ensuring AI behavior matches human goals.
60. **Explainability (XAI)** – Ability to understand why AI made a decision.

### Enterprise AI
61. **AI Copilot** – AI assistant that helps users perform tasks.
62. **Workflow Automation** – Using AI to automate business processes.
63. **Human-in-the-Loop (HITL)** – Human oversight in AI decision-making.
64. **Model Deployment** – Moving AI models into production.
65. **MLOps** – Practices for managing the AI lifecycle.
66. **Model Monitoring** – Tracking AI performance after deployment.
67. **Model Drift** – Decline in performance due to changing data patterns.
68. **A/B Testing** – Comparing different AI versions.
69. **AI Governance** – Policies and controls for responsible AI usage.
70. **Responsible AI** – Ethical and transparent AI development.

### Emerging AI Concepts (2025–2026)

71. **Context Engineering** – Designing and managing the information provided to AI for better results.
72. **Memory Systems** – Mechanisms allowing AI agents to retain information over time.
73. **Reasoning Model** – AI optimized for multi-step problem solving.
74. **Multimodal AI** – AI that understands text, images, audio, and video together.
75. **Vision Language Model (VLM)** – AI that processes both images and text.
76. **Model Context Protocol (MCP)** – Standardized way for AI agents to access tools and data sources.
77. **Synthetic Data** – Artificially generated training data.
78. **Digital Twin** – Virtual representation of a real-world system.
79. **World Model** – AI's internal understanding of environments and relationships.
80. **JEPA (Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture)** – A newer AI architecture focused on predicting abstract representations rather than raw outputs.
